1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites

The UK law for copyright is the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 and it protects original creations involving literary work, music, art, film and sound. There is also an additional law called the Copyright (Computer Programs) Regulations 1992 which extends protection to software. Under the UK law creative works are protected when they are created/published and so as soon as a website is posted online you are then protected immediately under copyright. However, it is still recommended to register with the UK Copyright Service because websites, especially with the highly accessible nature of information and images on the internet, are easily subject to abuses by the general public. Another important constraint that web designers and developers must be aware of is flexibility. When building a website it is important to note that users and viewers will stretch and distort the designs contained within your website and that they will access the website on different browsers and devices. Therefore, it is important to develop, if you are making a website for general use especially, with flexibility in mind - ensuring that the text will expand for example when a user zooms in on a page.

1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them

One issue that people with disabilities can face when trying to access websites is colour blindness and the design of the website not helping this. For example, two common colours which are difficult to differentiate between for those who are colour blind are red and green - web designers should perhaps try to be mindful therefore of the colours that they are using so that it is accessible for everyone. Blindness is another issue which can affect the ability of users to truly engage with a website and read all of the content. Web designers can try and ensure that their website is readable by screen readers such as Apple VoiceOver or Serotek Systems Access. Such software will read aloud the information on the screen while others might also create a braille display of the website content for a user. It is therefore beneficial for designers and developers to ensure that their website can be read and engage with such software.

1.7 Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites

The appropriate file types are ones such as HTML and CSS, which are used to input the content and then the style and positioning of that content retrospectively. Languages and file types for the back-end such as Javascript or even Python can also be used on a website to add more functionality to the website, perhaps such as creating a gallery which continuously scrolls through images on your landing page.
