1.4 Describe the risks that might impact digital projects - what causes digital projects to fail? Why?

There are two types of possible risk: internal (such as stakeholders or availability of resources) and external risk (regulatory changes or competitor activity). One key internal issue that could impact the successfulness of a digital project is not having a clearly defined minimum viable product (MVP), decided with yourself or with the client you are working for. The MVP can then be worked on further with updates, or in pre-release depending how far along schedule the development team is, but ultimately the MVP ensures that the project will be workable and live by a deadline - rather than allowing ‘scope creep’ - when there is uncontrolled growth in a projects scope and ambition. - https://www.thirdandgrove.com/four-risks-will-kill-your-digital-project-you-dont-even-know-look ; https://www.pmi.org/learning/library/top-five-causes-scope-creep-6675Another example of risk could relate to the actual technology itself rather than poor management, for example the deployment of a new operating system could effect whether your website or app is functional on the newest version.

1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects - how does GDPR impact digital projects?

One of the most crucial elements of the new GDPR, or general data protection regulation, is that it should be easier for people to withdraw their consent for data processing. The Information Commissioner’s Office stated that the main impact of GDPR was ‘Telling people they have the right to withdraw their consent at any time, and how to do this. It must be as easy to withdraw as it was to give consent. This means you will need to have simple and effective withdrawal mechanisms in place.’ - https://www.edelman.co.uk/insights/how-will-gdpr-impact-digital-marketing-professionals Some of the more specific rights of GDPR refer to the right to access which means that ‘individuals have the right to request access to their personal data and to ask how their data is used by the company after it has been gathered’ and the right to be forgotten which means that ‘if consumers are no longer customers, or if they withdraw their consent from a company to use their personal data, then they have the right to have their data deleted.’ - https://www.superoffice.com/blog/gdpr/ Therefore, much of GDPR gives individuals more right to have control over their data and restrict the way that corporations and businesses use that data, at the very least ensuring that the consumer has some idea about what their data is being used for or if there data is being used at all.
However, GDPR could perhaps have a positive impact for digital projects attempting to collect data for monetary gain but a negative impact for the consumer. Enza Iannopollo, a Forrester senior analyst for risk and security, states that ‘one of the changes we’re starting to see is the preference for first-party data, the data which comes directly from the consumer, because you have consent and the customer trusts you… because of that trust, they’re going to provide you with more accurate information, more relevant information - because with consent, if you’re transparent and I trust you, more likely I’m going to share more data with you.’ - https://www.zdnet.com/article/gdpr-how-europes-digital-privacy-rules-have-changed-everything/ So GDPR could actually mean that more accurate data is complied, which could be more useful or companies that benefit from collecting and creating data profiles.
